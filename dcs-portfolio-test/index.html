HBR Articles

While studying leaders and organizations that attract, develop, and manage talent so as to foster engagement and creativity, I found many insights in the pages of HBR.

How Pixar Fosters Collective Creativity

by Ed Catmull (September 2008). Pixar Animation Studios builds a rebel workforce from day one: During onboarding sessions, it highlights past company mistakes so that new hires will know it’s OK to fail. It also uses data during post mortems on movie productions to challenge assumptions and regularly hires prominent outsiders to inject fresh perspectives.

How to Hang On to Your High Potentials

by Claudio Fernández-Aráoz, Boris Groysberg, and Nitin Nohria (October 2011). To help fight conformity, companies can assess and develop employees’ curiosity—a good indicator of potential and useful trait for noncomformity.

Are You a High Potential?

by Douglas A. Ready, Jay A. Conger, and Linda A. Hill (June 2010)

How GE Teaches Teams to Lead Change

by Steven Prokesch (January 2009). HBR senior editor Steven Prokesch, who worked with me on “Rebel Talent,” put together this excellent feature on how to apply various methods to disrupt the status quo. The main focus is how to use executive development programs involving a business’ entire management team. Another approach is how to use language—introduce a new vocabulary—to change perspectives and priorities.

Managing Without Managers

by Ricardo Semler (September–October 1989)

Why My Former Employees Still Work for Me

by Ricardo Semler (January–February 1994). The author’s company, Semco, is a classic case of an organization that was structured to encourage nonconformity. These articles will help others follow suit.

Books

I’ve found inspiration in books from as far back as the 1950s that document in detail how and why companies create so much pressure to conform, and what can be done about it.

"The Organization Man"

by William H. Whyte (1956). A classic report on the soul-crushing pressure to conform to corporate norms as jobs shifted from small businesses to large corporations, and the nature of work changed.

"Reinventing Organizations: A Guide to Creating Organizations Inspired by the Next Stage of Human Consciousness"

by Frederic Laloux. Laloux argues that the way organizations are managed is out of date, and that employees long for workplaces that allow for authenticity, community, passion, and purpose. Full of examples.

"The Art of Being Unmistakable: A Collection of Essays About Making a Dent in The Universe"

by Srinivas Rao (2013). Too often we trap ourselves in conformity by holding on to our set way of looking at the world. This book is meant to shake us out of our status quo and disrupt how we see the world.

"The Man in the Gray Flannel Suit"

by Sloan Wilson (1955). This classic fictionalized case study tells the story of Tom Rath who ultimately breaks free from the pressure to conform, but it shows just how difficult it is to do that. Still relevant today.

"Bartleby, the Scrivener"

by Herman Melville (1853). For an unexpected critique of the American workplace, look to Melville’s masterpiece about a Wall Street lawyer’s clerk who rebels by simply no completing the tasks his boss has asked him to do by saying “I would prefer not to.” The boss, of course, doesn’t fire him.

Case Studies

The best way to dig into how companies actually create constructive nonconformity is through cases.

“Sun Hydraulics: Leading in Tough Times (A)”

by Linda A. Hill and Jennifer Suesse (2003). Too much nonconformity may result in chaos. This case depicts an organization that thought very carefully about the right balance between conformity and nonconformity and how to structure a workplace to achieve it.

“Pal's Sudden Service—Scaling an Organizational Model to Drive Growth”

by Gary P. Pisano, Francesca Gino, and Bradley R. Staats. In a company that applied many lean principles to drive efficiency and productivity, employees are still fully engaged as they experience variety in their jobs, and –through careful training- they are set up for success.

"The Morning Star Company: Self-Management at Work"

by Francesca Gino, Bradley R. Staats, Brian J. Hall, and Tiffany Y. Chang. (2013, updated 2016). One of my favorite cases, it depicts an organization that allows employees to play to their strengths and express themselves in their jobs. The result? A creative and productive workforce.

Other

Monkeys are adept at picking up social cues

New York Times piece on how monkeys behave like people in terms of making monkey-see-monkey-do cultural shifts. Conformity even happens with other species.

For Some Flight Attendants, Shtick Comes With the Safety Spiel

New York Times. One way airlines allow employees to be authentic (a solution against conformity) is to tinker with one of the most standards-based procedures in work.

I'm Quite Eccentric Within Accepted Societal Norms

The Onion. A funny look at the topic that always makes me laugh. Not much to learn here, except don’t wear a tie with a Hawaiian shirt.
