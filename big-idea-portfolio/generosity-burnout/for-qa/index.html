HBR Articles

Looking for more insights on what giving can do for—and to—you? Here’s some reading we’d suggest.

“Collaborative Overload”

by Rob Cross, Reb Rebele, and Adam Grant . Collaboration is indeed the answer to many pressing business challenges. But our research shows that more isn’t always better.

“In the Company of Givers and Takers ”

by Adam Grant. In this article, Adam shares some of his early thinking about giving and taking. He explains why your organization’s success depends on the generosity of employees and what you can do to support it.

“IDEO’s Culture of Helping ”

by Teresa Amabile, Colin M. Fisher, and Julianna Pillemer. The design firm IDEO has unleashed its creativity by making generosity the norm.

“The Limits of Empathy ”

by Adam Waytz. Empathy and generosity aren’t the same thing—but they have some things in common. In excess, both are exhausting, impair judgment, and hurt performance.

“Beating Burnout ”

by Monique Valcour. Though we’ve looked specifically at generosity burnout, all kinds of burnout are debilitating. Symptoms include distancing yourself from work, feelings of inefficacy, and profound physical, emotional, and cognitive fatigue. Valcour, a management coach, offers steps you can take toward recovery and prevention.

Books

We also recommend these deeper dives on the psychology of paying it forward—or not.

Give and Take: Why Helping Others Drives Our Success

by Adam Grant. In this foundational book on generosity, Adam explores why some givers climb to great heights while others become pushovers and doormats.

The No Asshole Rule: Building a Civilized Workplace and Surviving One That Isn’t

by Robert Sutton. The worst takers are sometimes known by a more vulgar term—and they can suck the life out of your team. This is one of our favorite books on how to deal with them.

Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make Everyone Smarter

by Liz Wiseman and Greg McKeown. When managers are givers, they see the potential in others and help them realize it. But this book makes a compelling case that giving too much direct help can actually stunt growth and limit team contributions.

A Path Appears: Transforming Lives, Creating Opportunity

by Nicholas D. Kristof and Sheryl WuDunn. In their fourth book together, this Pulitzer Prize–winning married couple examine how regular people make a difference–from fighting violence to preventing disease and fixing education. With inspiring examples, cutting-edge science, and practical recommendations, this book can change how you think about changing the world.

The Taking Tree: A Selfish Parody

by Shrill Travesty. Although Shel Silverstein’s children’s book The Giving Tree is a classic, we have a problem with it: It teaches kids to give to takers and to give selflessly until they have nothing left. This parody is a fun alternative in which the tree gets even.

Multimedia

Check out these ideas and tools—they might expand your capacity to give.

“Are You a Giver or a Taker?”

In a new TED talk, Adam explains how to build cultures of productive givers—and spot takers before it’s too late.

“IdeaCast: The Big Benefits of a Little Thanks”

Interview with Francesca Gino and Adam Grant, by Sarah Green Carmichael. Small expressions of gratitude have a powerful impact on our willingness to help others.

“Successful Givers, Toxic Takers, and the Life We Spend at Work”

In this interview with Krista Tippett for On Being, Adam discusses how his findings on generosity relate to work and life.

“Why Some People Are More Altruistic Than Others”

by Abigail Marsh. Why would anyone donate a kidney to a complete stranger? In this TED talk, a Georgetown psychologist explains that the amygdalae in the brains of extraordinary altruists may make them especially good at detecting people’s fear and distress.

“Research Suggests Generosity Is Hardwired into Our Brains”

by Shankar Vedantam. Because we’re wired to give, giving makes us happy—except when we’re forced to do it.

Assessment: Are You Paying It Forward to the Next Generation?

by Dan P. McAdams. Gauge how much your sense of success is tied to helping the next generation reach its goals.

Related Research

Here’s what other studies have discovered about the costs of giving.

““Well, I’m Tired of Tryin’!” ”

by by Mark C. Bolino et al. A pioneering study of what causes citizenship fatigue at work

“Organizational Citizenship Behavior and Career Outcomes: The Cost of Being a Good Citizen ”

by Diane M. Bergeron et al. If you’re not careful, time spent on being a good citizen at work can eat into task performance.

“Research: Yes, Being Helpful is Tiring”

by Klodiana Lanaj. We were intrigued by Lanaj’s findings, which suggest that responding to help requests at work is a double-edged sword.

“Being “Otherish”: Resolving the False Choice Between Personal and Prosocial Goals”

by Reb Rebele. In this chapter from the collection Flourishing in Life, Work and Careers, Reb reviews the challenges and opportunities of generosity and offers 10 strategies for becoming a more successful giver.
