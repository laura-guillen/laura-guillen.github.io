HBR Articles

Looking for more insights on what giving can do for you—and to you? Here’s some reading we’d suggest.

“Collaborative Overload”

by Rob Cross, Adam Grant, and Reb Rebele. Collaboration is indeed the answer to many pressing business challenges. But in our research, we’ve found that more isn’t always better.

“In the Company of Givers and Takers ”

by Adam Grant. In this article, Adam shares some of his early thinking about giving and taking. He explains why your organization’s success depends on the generosity of its employees and what you can do to support that.

“IDEO’s Culture of Helping ”

by Colin M. Fisher, Julianna Pillemer, and Teresa Amabile. The design firm IDEO has unleashed its creativity by making generosity the norm.

“The Limits of Empathy ”

by Adam Waytz. Empathy and generosity aren’t the same thing—but they have common friends. In excess, both are exhausting, impair judgment, and hurt performance.

“Beating Burnout ”

by Monique Valcour. Though we’ve looked specifically at generosity burnout, all kinds of burnout are debilitating. Symptoms include psychological distance from the work, feelings of inefficacy, and profound physical, emotional, and cognitive fatigue. Here are steps you can take toward recovery and prevention.

Books

We also recommend these deeper dives on the psychology of paying it forward—or not.

Give and Take: Why Helping Others Drives Our Success

by Adam Grant. This is Adam’s foundational book on generosity, where he began to explore why some givers climb to great heights and others become pushovers and doormats.

The No Asshole Rule: Building a Civilized Workplace and Surviving One That Isn’t

by Robert Sutton. The worst takers are sometimes known by a more vulgar name—and they can suck the life out of your team. This is one of our favorite books on how to get rid of them and how to deal with them.

Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make Everyone Smarter

by Liz Wiseman and Greg McKeown. To be a giver as a leader or manager is to see the potential in others and help them realize it. This book makes a compelling case that giving too much direct help can actually stunt growth and limit team contributions.

A Path Appears: Transforming Lives, Creating Opportunity

by Nicholas D. Kristof and Sheryl WuDunn. In their fourth book together, this Pulitzer Prize-winning married couple examines how regular people make a difference–from preventing disease and fixing education, to evaluating global aid and local charity, to fighting violence. In our experience, the combination of inspiring examples, cutting-edge science, and practical recommendations can change how people think about changing the world.

The Taking Tree

by Shrill Travesty. Although Shel Silverstein’s children’s book The Giving Tree is a classic, we have a problem with it. It teaches kids to give to takers, and to give selflessly until they literally have nothing left. This parody is a fun alternative where the tree gets even.

Multimedia

Check out these ideas and tools—they’ll expand your capacity to give.

“IdeaCast: The Big Benefits of a Little Thanks”

Interview with Francesca Gino and Adam Grant, by Sarah Green Carmichael. Small expressions of gratitude have a powerful impact on our willingness to help others.

“Successful Givers, Toxic Takers, and the Life We Spend at Work”

In this interview with Krista Tippett for On Being, Adam discusses how his work on generosity relates to work and life.

“Why Some People Are More Altruistic Than Others”

by Abigail Marsh. Why would anyone donate a kidney to a complete stranger? In this TED talk, Georgetown psychologist Abigail Marsh explains that the amygdala in extraordinary altruists’ brains may make them better than the rest of us at detecting people’s fear and distress.

“Research Suggests Generosity Is Hardwired into Our Brains”

by Shankar Vedantam. Because we’re wired to give, giving makes us happy—except when we’re forced to do it.

Assessment: Are You Paying It Forward to the Next Generation?

by Dan P. McAdams. Gauge how much of your success is tied up in helping the next generation reach its goals.

Related Research

Here’s what others have discovered in their studies about the costs of giving.

““Well, I’m Tired of Tryin’!” ”

by by Mark C. Bolino et al. A pioneering study of what causes citizenship fatigue at work.

“Organizational Citizenship Behavior and Career Outcomes: The Cost of Being a “Good Citizen””

by Diane M. Bergeron et al. If you’re not careful, time spent on being a good citizen at work can eat into task performance.

“Research: Yes, Being Helpful is Tiring”

by Klodiana Lanaj. We were intrigued by these recent findings, which suggest that responding to help requests at work is a double-edged sword.

“Being “Otherish”: Resolving the False Choice Between Personal and Prosocial Goals”

by Reb Rebele. In this chapter of the edited volume Flourishing in Work, Life, and Careers, Reb reviews the challenges and opportunities of generosity and offers ten strategies for becoming a more successful giver.
