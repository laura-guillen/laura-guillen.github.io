HBR Articles

While studying leaders and organizations that attract, develop, and manage talent so as to spark engagement and creativity, I found many insights in the pages of HBR.

“How Pixar Fosters Collective Creativity”

by Ed Catmull (September 2008). Pixar Animation Studios builds a rebel workforce from day one: During onboarding sessions, it highlights past company mistakes so that new hires will know it’s OK to fail.

“Are You a High Potential?”

by Douglas A. Ready, Jay A. Conger, and Linda A. Hill (June 2010)

“How to Hang On to Your High Potentials”

by Claudio Fernández-Aráoz, Boris Groysberg, and Nitin Nohria (October 2011)

To help fight conformity, companies can assess and develop employees’ curiosity — a good indicator of potential.

“How GE Teaches Teams to Lead Change”

by Steven Prokesch (January 2009). HBR senior editor Steven Prokesch, who worked with me on “Rebel Talent,” put together this excellent feature on how to use language to disrupt the status quo.

“Managing Without Managers”

by Ricardo Semler (September–October 1989)

“Why My Former Employees Still Work for Me”

by Ricardo Semler (January–February 1994)

The author’s company, Semco, is a classic case of an organization that was structured to encourage nonconformity. These articles will help others follow suit.

Books

I’ve found inspiration in books from as far back as the 1850s that document in detail how and why companies create pressure to conform and what can be done to combat it.

The Organization Man

by William H. Whyte (1956). A report on the soul-crushing conformity of corporate norms as jobs shifted from small businesses to large corporations and the nature of work changed.

Reinventing Organizations: A Guide to Creating Organizations Inspired by the Next Stage of Human Consciousness

by Frederic Laloux (2014). Laloux argues that the way organizations are managed is out-of-date and that employees long for workplaces where authenticity, community, passion, and purpose are allowed. Full of examples.

The Art of Being Unmistakable: A Collection of Essays About Making a Dent in the Universe

by Srinivas Rao (2013). This book is meant to shake us out of our status quo and disrupt how we see the world.

Collective Genius: The Art and Practice of Leading Innovation

by Linda A. Hill, Greg Brandeau, Emily Truelove, and Kent Lineback (2014). The powerhouse team of authors focuses more on innovation than engagement, but the message is clearly similar to that of “Rebel Talent”: Leaders can learn to unleash and harness employees’ talents and creativity.

“Bartleby, the Scrivener”

by Herman Melville (1853). For an unusual critique of the American workplace, look to Melville’s masterpiece about a Wall Street clerk who rebels by not completing the tasks his boss assigns him, saying, “I would prefer not to.” (The boss doesn't fire him, though.)

Case Studies

The best way to learn how to foster constructive nonconformity is to dig into how actual companies did so.

“Sun Hydraulics: Leading in Tough Times (A)”

by Linda A. Hill and Jennifer M. Suesse (2003). This case depicts an organization that thought very carefully about the right balance of conformity and nonconformity and how to structure a workplace to achieve it.

“Pal's Sudden Service — Scaling an Organizational Model to Drive Growth”

by Gary P. Pisano, Francesca Gino, and Bradley R. Staats (2016). In a company that uses lean principles to drive efficiency and productivity, employees remain fully engaged because they experience variety in their jobs, while careful training sets them up for success.

“The Morning Star Company: Self-Management at Work”

by Francesca Gino and Bradley R. Staats (2013). This organization — one of my favorite examples — allows employees to express themselves on the job and play to their strengths. The result? A creative and productive workforce.

Other Articles

“Monkeys Are Adept at Picking Up Social Cues, Research Shows”

by Pam Belluck (New York Times, 2013). Conformity occurs among other species, too.

“For Some Flight Attendants, Shtick Comes With the Safety Spiel”

by Zach Schonbrun (New York Times, 2016). Airlines let employees be authentic by allowing them to tinker with one of the most standardized procedures in the industry.

“I'm Quite Eccentric Within Accepted Societal Norms”

by Martin Grossman (The Onion, 2007). A funny take on the topic. Not much to learn here, except don’t wear a tie with a Hawaiian shirt.
